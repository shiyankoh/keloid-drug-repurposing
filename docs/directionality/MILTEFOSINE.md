# MILTEFOSINE — Directionality Research

## Drug Class
Phospholipid analog (alkylphosphocholine); originally developed as an antiprotozoal (leishmaniasis). Acts as an Akt/PKB inhibitor with membrane-disrupting properties.

## ChEMBL ID
CHEMBL125 (mechanism listed as "Unknown" — described as involving lipid interaction, cytochrome c oxidase inhibition, and apoptosis-like cell death)

## Gene-by-Gene Directionality

| Gene | Direction | Confidence | Net Effect | Rationale |
|------|-----------|------------|------------|-----------|
| STAT3 | activator | medium | unfavorable | Two independent studies show miltefosine enhances STAT3 activation. In CVB3 antiviral model, STAT3 activation was essential for drug efficacy (STAT3 knockdown abolished activity). In megakaryocyte differentiation, miltefosine activates JAK2/STAT3 at 10-40 μM, confirmed by ruxolitinib blockade. Mechanism: Akt inhibition → enhanced IL-6/IFN responses → STAT3 activation. |
| SMAD3 | inhibitor | medium | beneficial | Miltefosine suppresses TGFβ1/Smad3 signaling pathway in Pten-null T-ALL. Mechanism: pT308-Akt inhibition → TGFβ1/Smad3 suppression → β-catenin degradation. Evidence from leukemia blasts, not fibroblasts, but pathway is directly relevant to keloid fibrosis. |

## Summary

Miltefosine presents a mixed directionality profile for keloid targets. As an Akt inhibitor, it suppresses TGFβ1/SMAD3 signaling — a core pro-fibrotic pathway in keloids — which is beneficial. However, the same Akt inhibition paradoxically enhances STAT3 activation via compensatory IL-6 and JAK2 signaling, which is unfavorable since STAT3 is pro-keloid. No keloid-specific or fibrosis-specific studies exist for miltefosine; all evidence comes from virology and oncology models. The drug's primary clinical use remains antiprotozoal (visceral/cutaneous leishmaniasis).
